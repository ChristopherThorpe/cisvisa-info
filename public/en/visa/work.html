Work in the USA

US work visas usually require a US firm to sponsor your visa. Beyond this, though, work visas are one of the most complex areas of immigration law.

Key Facts
  • These visas are generally tied to a specific employer. This means that you need to have your job arranged before you come to the US, and you must update your visa if you want to change jobs. If you lose your job, you can lose your visa and have to leave the country.
  • There are many different visa types available. They differ in eligibility (what you can apply for) and rights (what they let you do).
  • Eligibility is usually driven by your level of education. The more education you have, the more choices you have.
  • Your rights differ as well. Most allow you to bring your family, but only a subset offer a path to citizenship.

Visa types
  • New job: H1-B, E1, E3, EB-2, TN
  • Within your company: L, E-2
  • University student or recent graduate: J (intern), J (trainee), J (summer work)
  • Short-term: J (Au pair), J (camp counselor)
  • Academic: J (professor), J (scholar), J (teacher), J (specialist)
