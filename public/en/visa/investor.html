Investors

These visas are for people who are going to invest a substantial amount of money in a US-based business. If you qualify, they're very attractive, but they're complicated, so we'll start with examples.

Examples

  • A woman from Belgium opened a consulting practice in the United States. She invested $75,000 to purchase office equipment, lease an office, and operate her business. She got an E-2 visa.
  • A large UK company decided to build a major processing plant in Texas. The director of the business unit and dozens of essential employees qualified for E-2 visas.
  • A Nigerian man invested $1,000,000 to build a new restaurant in the United States and hired 10 new full-time employees. He and his family were granted EB-5 "green cards".

Key facts

  • There are two main types of investor visas. There are exceptions to these guidelines, but in general your options depend on how much money you're prepared to invest.
For investments of $50,000 or more, you're a candidate for an E-2 visa.

  • These visas give you terrific priveleges. You can bring your family, you can sponsor essential employees, and your spouse can work.
  • You're expected to manage the business, and you have to show that you're qualified to do this, but you don't have to own it. In other words, you might be coming to run part of an existing company, as an employee.
  • Some countries are not eligible.
  • We've seen these work for as little as $20,000 in very unusual cases, but the odds are against you with smaller investments.
For investments above $500,000, creating 10 or more new full-time jobs, you could qualify for an EB-5 green card.

  • You go directly to a green card. This gives you all the rights of a permanent resident.
  • There are no country restrictions. It doesn't matter where you're from.
