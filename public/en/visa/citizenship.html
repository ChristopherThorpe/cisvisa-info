Citizenship

The most common way to become a citizen is to first become a permanent resident (green card holder), then to go through a process called naturalization. Here are key points about that process:

  • The first requirement is time. If you're married to a citizen, you'll need to have held your green card and lived in the US for the past 3 years. You can only have left the country (for any reason, such as travel) in 18 of those 36 months. If you're not married to a citizen, it takes 5 years, during which you have to be here for 30 of those 60 months.
  • The next one is simpler: you must be at least 18 years old.
  • You'll also be tested on two subjects. First, you must read, write and speak English. Second, you'll have to know facts about U.S. history and government (known as civics). Exceptions may apply for persons over 50 or with certain disabilities. See below for more information about these tests.
There are several other special situations which can lead directly to citizenship. These rules are complex and likely require an attorney's help.

  • Special rules apply for families of members of the US armed forces, including when the service member died in combat.
  • Many children born outside the United States qualify for US citizenship if one or both biological or adoptive parents is a US citizen.
